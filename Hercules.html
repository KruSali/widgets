The Hero Hercules

Page 1/6: Character and Birth

üí™The Greatest Hero of Greece

Hercules was the greatest hero of Greece (except in Athens). He was the strongest man on earth and had supreme self-confidence, considering himself an equal to the gods. He even helped the gods conquer the Giants. However, he was not intelligent. His thinking was limited to how to defeat the monster before him. What made him great was his **greatness of soul** and his willingness to always atone for his wrongdoings.

‚ö°A Remarkable Birth

Hercules was born in Thebes. He was the son of **Zeus** and **Princess Alcmena**. Zeus visited her disguised as her husband, Amphitryon, who was away at war. The jealous Hera tried to prevent his birth but failed.

üêçFirst Heroic Deed

When Hercules was a baby, Hera sent two great snakes to his cradle to kill him. But the infant laughed with delight and strangled them with his bare hands. All knew then that the child was destined for great things.

Tragedy and Atonement

Page 2/6: Madness and Penance

üò†Youthful Mistakes

Hercules was very emotional. In his youth, he unintentionally killed his music master with his lute in a fit of rage after being reprimanded. He later lived with shepherds and performed his first great deed: killing the Thespian lion, whose skin he wore as a cloak and whose head he used as a helmet.

ü§ØHera's Madness

He later married **Princess Megara** and had three sons. But Hera, never forgetting her grudge, sent madness upon him. In his insanity, he killed his wife and children. When his sanity returned, he was overwhelmed with grief and intended to kill himself.

ü§ùThe Friendship of Theseus

As he was about to commit suicide, his friend **Theseus** stopped him, grasping his bloodstained hands to share his guilt. Theseus persuaded Hercules to live and fight against his fate. Hercules then journeyed to Delphi to seek guidance from Apollo on how to purify himself.

The Twelve Labors (Part 1)

Page 3/6: Labors 1-6

The priestess at Delphi ordered Hercules to go to his cousin, **King Eurystheus** of Mycenae, and submit to his commands as penance. The cowardly and envious Eurystheus gave him twelve dangerous and nearly impossible tasks.

üìúLabors 1-6

  • Kill the lion of Nemea: A beast no weapons could wound. Hercules choked it to death.
  • Kill the Hydra of Lerna: A nine-headed creature that grew two new heads for each one cut off. He seared the necks with a burning brand to prevent regrowth.
  • Bring back alive the stag with golden horns: Sacred to Artemis. He hunted it for a whole year before succeeding.
  • Capture a great boar from Mount Erymanthus: He chased it until it was exhausted, then drove it into deep snow and trapped it.
  • Clean the Augean stables in a single day: Stalls of thousands of cattle that had not been cleaned for years. He diverted two rivers to flow through them.
  • Drive away the Stymphalian birds: A plague of birds. He was helped by Athena to drive them from their coverts, and then shot them.

The Twelve Labors (Part 2)

Page 4/6: Labors 7-12

üìúLabors 7-12

  • Go to Crete and fetch the beautiful savage bull that Poseidon had given Minos.
  • Get the man-eating mares of King Diomedes of Thrace. He slew Diomedes first and then drove off the mares.
  • Bring back the girdle of Hippolyta, Queen of the Amazons. She was willing to give it, but Hera incited the Amazons to attack. Hercules killed Hippolyta and took the girdle.
  • Bring back the cattle of Geryon, a three-bodied monster. He journeyed to the western end of the world, setting up two great rocks (the pillars of Hercules) as a memorial.
  • Bring back the Golden Apples of the Hesperides. He tricked **Atlas** into getting the apples for him while he held up the heavens.
  • Bring Cerberus up from Hades. Pluto gave him permission, provided he used no weapons. He forced the three-headed dog to submit with his bare hands and carried him to Mycenae, then returned him.

Adventures After the Labors

Page 5/6: The Story of Alcestis

ü§ºThe Fight with Antaeus

After the labors, Hercules continued his adventures. He defeated **Antaeus**, a Giant wrestler who was invincible as long as he touched the earth. Hercules lifted him into the air and strangled him.

‚ù§Ô∏èThe Rescue of Alcestis

On his way to get the man-eating mares, Hercules visited his friend **Admetus**, who was mourning because his wife, **Alcestis**, was about to die in his place. But Admetus, to be a good host, hid his grief and welcomed Hercules.

Hercules, unaware, drank and made merry until a servant revealed the truth. Filled with remorse, Hercules decided to help his friend. He ambushed **Death** and wrestled him to bring Alcestis back to life, which he successfully did.

The End of the Hero

Page 6/6: Death and Ascension

üíîA New Transgression

Hercules continued to make mistakes. He killed King Eurytus's son to avenge an insult. As punishment, Zeus sent him to be a slave to **Queen Omphale** of Lydia for three years.

üî•The Poisoned Robe

After his freedom, he conquered Eurytus's city and took **Princess Iole** captive. His wife, **Deianira**, became jealous. She anointed a robe with the blood of the Centaur Nessus, believing it was a love charm, and sent it to Hercules.

But the blood was a deadly poison. When Hercules put on the robe, he was seized with unbearable pain. Knowing death was near, he built his own funeral pyre on Mount Oeta and asked **Philoctetes** to light it, giving him his bow and arrows in return.

‚ú®Ascension to Olympus

As the flames consumed his body, Hercules was taken to heaven. There he was reconciled to Hera, married **Hebe**, the Goddess of Youth, and lived in peace among the blessed immortals forever.
